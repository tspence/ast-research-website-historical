AST OFFERS CUSTOMERS FUTURE-READY PRODUCTS TO MEET THE YEAR 2000 TRANSITION

Latest AST Servers, Commercial Desktops and Mobile PCs Recognize and Register Coming Millennium

IRVINE, Calif., Sept. 3, 1996 -- Recognizing that the turn of the century poses significant challenges for the information technology industry, AST Computer (NASDAQ:ASTA) today announced that its line of commercial desktops, servers and notebooks are equipped to deal with the onset of the year 2000. All Bravo commercial desktops introduced after April 1996, Manhattan S6200 and Manhattan CommercePRO servers introduced after July 1996, and notebook PCs introduced after May 1, 1996 are next century-compliant.

"We believe that a large percentage of businesses today are taking a 'wait and see' attitude and have not yet begun to address the technological issues of what a move to the next century really means," said Dan Sheppard, vice president, worldwide product marketing. "The year 2000 is real and we are committed to providing technology solutions that address the challenges users face as they move into the next millennium."

Moving Into the Next Century
"In the 1960's and 70's computers were living-room size, however, they had very little storage space. Rather than waste valuable and costly space, engineers programmed the computers not to store the first two digits of the year," said Sheppard. "This means that when the next millennium arrives, the internal clocks of mainframes and other computing devices will change from 12/31/99 to 1/1/00, meaning that systems will not be able to distinguish between the year 1900 and 2000 -- potentially throwing systems into chaos."

AST however, has designed its current PC lineup to handle the year 2000 smoothly and efficiently. AST is actively replacing two-digit date fields, which cannot distinguish between the years 1900 and 2000, with four-digit fields by incorporating a four-digit year date code into the BIOS of its systems.

AST recognizes that at risk for the year 2000 are proprietary applications, in which developers created their own date-related code instead of using the year 2000 compliant date set supplied by vendors.

"Imagine that your insurance policy is up for renewal in the year 2001," noted Sheppard. "If there is an error in the program due to a spreadsheet macro unable to issue a forecast beyond 2000, then a faulty program might recognize the last two digits of the year as 1901 and throw the policy out."

Additional information on the significance of the impending change to the next century can be found on the Year 2000 web site at http://www.year2000.com.
