AST Research Helps Complete Computer Data Network
Between California’s Community & State Colleges

 

IRVINE, Calif., Sept. 9, 1997 – ASTÒ Research Inc. today helped further the Information Superhighway links between California’s community and state college systems by donating 20 server PC systems to the Chancellor’s Office of the California Community Colleges.

The 20 powerful AST ManhattanÒ server systems are expected to play a vital role in the California Community Colleges’ massive technology initiative, connecting its data network of 107 community colleges with the 23 campuses of the California State University system.

The server PCs were presented by Scott Bower, AST senior vice president, Americas sales and marketing, at a meeting of the Board of Governors of the California Community Colleges held at Coast Community College’s Garden Grove, Calif. campus.

"We are investing in our state’s future by helping to enhance learning through networking and communication," said Mr. Bower. "AST believes strongly in supporting higher education. As such, we expect these systems will enable students at many of California’s colleges to have access to vital computer resources."

The donation resulted from collaboration between Dr. Larry Toy, director of human resource development and system advancement for the Chancellor’s office; Joyce Arntson, faculty member, Irvine Valley College; and Dennis Holden, AST director of sales, Southwest region.

 

"This generous donation from AST will greatly assist our continuing efforts to infuse technology systemwide," said Thomas J. Nussbaum, chancellor, California Community Colleges. "We are developing major initiatives to provide computer linkages that include new courseware and on-line directories. With AST’s contribution, we will be able to enhance our capacity for these applications to help assure student success into the 21st century."

Student impact throughout the state from AST’s donation is expected to be significant, with a minimum of 44 on-line courses expected to be launched at both community and state colleges during the current academic year. Once up and running, students will be able to take these courses at the time and place of their choice, thereby adding valuable flexibility to the class schedules of many students.
